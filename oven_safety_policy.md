# Oven Safety Policy

## 1. Purpose

This policy defines the mandatory safety rules for using lab ovens, PCB reflow ovens, curing ovens, bake-out ovens, and related trays or fixtures in the lab. It is intended to prevent burns, fire, electrical shock, fume exposure, chemical exposure, eye injury, equipment damage, and property damage.

Every employee, contractor, intern, visitor, or other person who operates, assists with, cleans, maintains, loads, unloads, or observes use of the oven must follow this policy and sign the acknowledgement before participating in oven work.

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to:

- Heating PCBs, assemblies, solder paste, adhesives, epoxies, conformal coatings, parts, fixtures, test coupons, or approved lab materials in an oven.
- Reflow, curing, bake-out, drying, preheating, thermal testing, cooldown, cleanup, troubleshooting, and routine maintenance.
- Handling hot oven doors, chambers, trays, racks, fixtures, PCBs, components, solder paste, flux, adhesives, and oven waste.
- Anyone working near the oven while it is powered, heating, operating, cooling, being cleaned, or being serviced.

This policy does not cover heat-set insert installation, which is covered by the Heat Insert Tool Safety Policy. It does not replace the Soldering Iron Safety Policy for hand soldering or the ComMarker Omni X Laser Safety Policy for PCB laser work. When an oven process overlaps with those policies, the more specific policy and the more conservative control must be followed.

## 3. Conservative Safety Assumption

The person handling the oven shall treat every oven chamber, door, tray, rack, fixture, PCB, component, solder paste deposit, adhesive, and recently heated item as hot unless they have personally verified that it is cool.

The operator shall treat oven work as a combined heat, fire, electrical, chemical, fume, sharp-object, and stored-energy hazard. Familiarity with a routine reflow, cure, or bake profile does not remove the need for PPE, ventilation, housekeeping, monitoring, and active attention.

If the oven is damaged, producing smoke from the chamber, cord, or control panel, overheating abnormally, failing to regulate temperature, tripping power, leaking fumes into the room, missing guards, or producing an abnormal odor, it must be removed from service until inspected and cleared.

## 4. Main Ways a Person Could Be Hurt

Employees must understand that injury can occur during setup, loading, heating, unloading, cooldown, cleanup, and troubleshooting. The principal hazards are:

1. **Burns from hot oven surfaces and parts.** Oven doors, chambers, heaters, trays, racks, fixtures, PCBs, components, solder, and recently heated parts can burn skin.
2. **Burns from molten solder or hot chemicals.** Molten solder, solder paste, hot flux, hot adhesive, hot epoxy, and overheated residues can stick to skin and continue burning.
3. **Eye injury.** Solder paste or flux can spatter, components can shift, brittle parts can crack, clipped leads can eject, and tools or trays can slip.
4. **Fume exposure.** Flux, solder paste, adhesives, conformal coatings, solder mask, PCB residues, heated plastics, and overheated materials can release irritating or hazardous fumes, odors, smoke, and particulates.
5. **Fire.** Oven heaters, failed temperature control, incorrect profiles, paper, cardboard, wipes, packaging, plastic scraps, solvents, aerosols, dust, or unattended abnormal operation can cause smoke or fire.
6. **Electrical shock.** Damaged cords, wet surfaces, exposed wiring, improper repairs, modified controllers, poor grounding, or liquids near powered equipment can create shock hazards.
7. **Cuts and punctures.** Sharp PCB edges, cut leads, metal trays, racks, fixtures, glass, broken parts, and tools can cut or puncture skin.
8. **Unexpected process behavior.** Wrong temperature, wrong dwell time, wrong profile, wrong material, incorrect timer, software error, controller failure, or blocked airflow can cause overheating, part failure, smoke, or fire.
9. **Chemical exposure.** Solder paste, flux, uncured adhesive, conformal coating, cleaning solvents, isopropyl alcohol, and contaminated wipes can irritate skin, eyes, and lungs.
10. **Lead or metal exposure.** Leaded solder paste, solder residue, contaminated trays, dross, wipes, and dirty hands can expose users to lead or other metals if ingested or transferred to food, drinks, phones, or personal items.
11. **Ergonomic strain.** Lifting trays, reaching into ovens, holding awkward fixtures, and repeated loading or unloading can cause strain or fatigue.
12. **Complacency risk.** Injuries are more likely when an operator assumes a tray has cooled, uses unknown materials, overloads an oven, skips ventilation, or leaves an abnormal process running.

## 5. Operator Responsibilities

### 5.1 Person Handling the Oven

The person handling the oven shall:

- Complete required training before use.
- Follow this policy, the manufacturer's instructions, the lab operating procedure, and applicable material safety data sheets.
- Review and follow related lab policies when the work involves soldering, solder paste, laser-processed PCBs, flux, adhesives, solvents, coated boards, or 3D-printed parts.
- Confirm the oven, work area, power cord, controller, temperature profile, timer, ventilation, material, tray, fixture, and PPE are safe before use.
- Use fume extraction or local ventilation when heating flux, solder paste, adhesives, coatings, contaminated boards, plastics, or any material that can emit fumes.
- Keep combustible materials and liquids away from the oven.
- Monitor oven operations according to this policy.
- Stop work immediately if unsafe conditions exist.
- Report injuries, near misses, smoke events, fire events, electrical faults, abnormal odors, damaged equipment, temperature-control failures, ventilation failures, or unsafe conditions.
- Remove unsafe equipment from service.

### 5.2 Observers and Visitors

Observers and visitors may approach the oven only with permission from the person handling the equipment. They must avoid hot surfaces, hot trays, fumes, sharp edges, oven doors, and active work areas. Visitors may not operate the oven unless trained and authorized.

## 6. Authorization and Training

No person may operate the oven independently until all of the following are complete:

- Review of this policy and signature of the acknowledgement.
- Review of the current manufacturer manual and lab operating procedure.
- Review of the Soldering Iron Safety Policy before oven work involving solder paste, flux, leaded solder, solder residue, or soldering chemicals.
- Review of the ComMarker Omni X Laser Safety Policy before heating laser-processed, coated, or residue-bearing PCB materials created by the laser process.
- Review of the Bambu Lab P2S 3D Printer Safety Policy before heating 3D-printed materials or printer-related plastics.
- Hands-on training by a person already competent with the specific oven, materials, profiles, trays, fixtures, and procedure.
- Demonstrated ability to inspect the oven, verify ventilation, select a safe profile, load and unload safely, monitor heating, shut down the oven, handle hot parts, clean the area, and respond to burns, fumes, smoke, or fire.
- Demonstrated understanding of hot surfaces, material limits, fume hazards, fire prevention, electrical hazards, chemical handling, required PPE, and incident reporting.

Refresher review is required after any incident, near miss, equipment change, material change, process change, temperature-profile change, or extended period without use.

## 7. Approved Materials and Ventilation

Only approved materials may be heated. The operator must confirm the material identity, temperature limit, required ventilation, safe dwell time, oven profile, and compatibility with the oven before heating.

Fume extraction or local ventilation must be used whenever heating flux, solder paste, adhesives, conformal coatings, solder mask, contaminated boards, plastics, unknown residues, or any material that produces odor, smoke, fumes, or particulates. Ventilation must be operating before heating and must continue as needed after heating to clear fumes and odors.

The following are prohibited unless specifically approved by the person responsible for the lab or equipment:

- Heating unknown, unlabeled, contaminated, wet, oily, chemically treated, visibly degraded, or incompatible materials.
- Heating materials above their rated temperature, decomposition temperature, manufacturer limit, or approved lab process limit.
- Heating PVC, vinyl, halogenated plastics, unknown plastics, unknown coatings, unknown adhesives, batteries, sealed containers, pressurized items, or aerosol-contaminated items.
- Heating food, drink, personal items, or non-lab materials.
- Using a lab oven for food after it has been used for PCBs, solder paste, plastics, adhesives, chemicals, or lab materials.
- Heating solvents, solvent-soaked wipes, aerosols, flammable liquids, or uncapped chemical containers.
- Using leaded solder paste or lead-contaminated materials without approved lead-handling controls.
- Using the oven for materials that require ventilation, filtration, temperature accuracy, atmosphere control, or fire controls the lab setup cannot safely provide.

If a material produces strong odor, visible smoke, charring, bubbling, unexpected melting, flame, or abnormal reaction, stop the process if safe to do so and report the issue.

## 8. Required Engineering Controls

The following controls must be in place before oven operation:

- A stable, temperature-controlled oven suitable for the task.
- A stable, level, heat-resistant, nonflammable installation surface.
- Oven trays, racks, fixtures, and clamps suitable for the expected temperature and material.
- Fume extraction or local ventilation when required by this policy, the material, or the lab procedure.
- Adequate clearance for ventilation, door swing, loading, unloading, and emergency access.
- Adequate lighting and clear access around the oven.
- Safe cable routing so cords cannot create a trip hazard or contact hot surfaces.
- A timer, profile controller, or process control method that prevents unintended extended heating.
- A suitable fire extinguisher visible and accessible in the lab.
- Approved containers for solder paste waste, failed boards, contaminated wipes, chemical waste, and sharp waste.
- Labeled chemical containers with lids closed when not in active use.

If any required control is missing or defective, oven operation must not begin.

## 9. Required PPE

Minimum PPE for normal oven operation:

- Safety glasses.
- Heat-resistant gloves when loading, unloading, or handling hot trays, fixtures, PCBs, parts, or recently heated assemblies.
- Nitrile gloves when handling flux, solder paste, adhesives, solvents, contaminated boards, uncured chemicals, lead-containing materials, or cleaning chemicals.
- Cut-resistant gloves when handling sharp PCBs, sharp fixtures, broken parts, or sheet metal if they do not create an entanglement or dexterity hazard.

Operators must secure long hair, loose clothing, jewelry, lanyards, hoodie strings, and dangling items before using the oven.

PPE does not make it safe to touch oven heaters, molten solder, hot trays, energized parts, chemical residue, or unventilated fumes.

## 10. Pre-Use Checklist

Before using the oven, the operator shall:

1. Confirm they are trained and not impaired, distracted, or rushed.
2. Confirm the oven area is clean, stable, dry, and free of unnecessary combustible material.
3. Inspect the oven, controller, door, gasket, tray, rack, fixture, power cord, plug, and temperature controls for damage.
4. Confirm fume extraction or local ventilation is operating when required.
5. Confirm the material, PCB, part, solder paste, adhesive, coating, tray, and fixture are approved for the process.
6. Confirm the selected temperature, dwell time, oven profile, and cooldown plan are appropriate.
7. Confirm the workpiece is not powered and does not contain a battery unless covered by a specific safe procedure.
8. Confirm the workpiece does not contain sealed or pressure-sensitive components that could rupture.
9. Confirm the fire extinguisher is accessible.
10. Confirm safety glasses and other required PPE are worn.
11. Confirm tools are available for safe loading, unloading, and hot-part handling.

If anything is uncertain, stop and do not operate the oven until the safe setup and procedure are clear.

## 11. Operating Rules

Operators shall follow these rules during every oven task:

- Use the oven only for approved lab materials and approved temperature profiles.
- Do not use a lab oven for food or personal items.
- Do not overload the oven or block airflow unless the manufacturer and lab procedure allow the loading pattern.
- Use trays, racks, and fixtures that are stable at the process temperature.
- Keep paper, cardboard, wipes, solvents, aerosols, plastic bags, loose packaging, and unnecessary combustibles away from the oven.
- Do not place sealed containers, batteries, pressurized items, unknown chemicals, or flammable liquids in the oven.
- Do not heat materials that can produce hazardous fumes unless the required exhaust or filtration is operating.
- Monitor the start of each heating cycle and remain able to respond to abnormal conditions.
- Do not leave a higher-risk, new, untested, or fume-generating process unattended.
- Use heat-resistant gloves or tools to load and unload hot trays, racks, fixtures, PCBs, or parts.
- Open the oven cautiously to avoid hot air, fumes, and unstable trays.
- Do not reach into the oven with bare hands.
- Do not bypass, modify, or ignore thermostats, timers, interlocks, alarms, door switches, fuses, guards, or safety controls.
- Do not use the oven if temperature control is unreliable, the door does not close correctly, fumes leak into the room, or the power cord is damaged.

Stop the process immediately if there is smoke, flame, strong odor, abnormal temperature rise, controller fault, ventilation failure, electrical fault, or unexpected material behavior.

## 12. Lead, Solder Paste, Adhesive, and Chemical Controls

If solder paste, flux, leaded solder, adhesives, epoxies, conformal coating, or cleaning chemicals are used:

- Review the applicable safety data sheet before use.
- Treat solder paste, residue, contaminated trays, used wipes, failed boards, and dirty gloves as chemically contaminated.
- Treat leaded solder paste, leaded solder residue, dross, and contaminated wipes as lead-contaminated.
- Keep chemicals capped, labeled, and stored correctly when not in active use.
- Keep food, drinks, gum, cosmetics, contact lenses, and personal items away from the oven area.
- Wash hands after handling materials and before eating, drinking, or leaving the lab.
- Do not use compressed air to blow solder paste, dust, residue, or debris into the room.
- Dispose of contaminated waste according to lab waste procedures.

Lead-free solder paste and cured adhesives still require ventilation, gloves when appropriate, hand washing, and good housekeeping.

## 13. Shutdown and Post-Use Procedure

After using the oven, the operator shall:

1. Stop the heating cycle and confirm the oven is no longer actively heating unless a controlled cooldown is part of the procedure.
2. Power off the oven when required by the lab procedure unless another trained operator is immediately taking over.
3. Allow the oven chamber, trays, fixtures, parts, PCBs, and assemblies to cool before handling or storage.
4. Continue ventilation as needed until fumes and odors are cleared.
5. Return trays, fixtures, tools, chemicals, and materials to their proper storage locations.
6. Dispose of failed boards, solder paste waste, contaminated wipes, chemical waste, sharp debris, and PCB debris properly.
7. Clean the oven area without spreading dust, paste, residue, or debris into the room.
8. Wash hands after cleanup.

Do not store hot trays, hot PCBs, hot fixtures, or hot parts in drawers, bins, bags, cabinets, or near combustible material.

## 14. Maintenance and Troubleshooting

Routine maintenance may be performed only by trained users. Non-routine maintenance, electrical repair, controller repair, calibration, heating-element replacement, oven disassembly, cord replacement, or repair must be performed only by a person competent for that task under a specific safe procedure.

Before maintenance, the operator shall:

- Power off and unplug equipment when required.
- Allow hot components to cool unless heat is required by the approved procedure.
- Use proper tools and PPE.
- Keep liquids away from electrical parts.
- Preserve ventilation controls when cleaning residues that may generate dust or fumes.

During troubleshooting:

- Never touch exposed energized parts.
- Never bypass grounding, fuses, thermostats, interlocks, alarms, timers, or temperature controls.
- Never operate an oven with a damaged door, broken temperature control, cracked insulation, exposed wiring, unreliable fan, damaged cord, or repeated over-temperature behavior.
- Tag unsafe equipment out of service and report it.

## 15. Housekeeping and Waste

The oven area must be kept clean and orderly.

Operators shall:

- Keep the area free of loose paper, cardboard, packaging, wipes, foam, plastic bags, and unnecessary combustibles.
- Keep sharp PCB edges, clipped leads, broken parts, trays, and fixtures contained or stored safely.
- Keep solder paste, flux, adhesives, cleaners, solvents, and chemicals capped and labeled.
- Keep wet items and liquids away from powered equipment.
- Clean trays, fixtures, and work surfaces using approved methods.
- Dispose of solder paste waste, lead-containing waste, contaminated wipes, adhesive waste, chemical waste, PCB debris, and failed parts according to lab procedures.

Do not blow dust, paste, dried residue, plastic debris, or PCB debris into the room with compressed air.

## 16. Emergency Procedures

### 16.1 Burns

- Move away from the hot source.
- Cool the burn with clean cool water according to first-aid procedure.
- Do not pull hardened solder, plastic, adhesive, or other stuck material from skin.
- Report the injury immediately.
- Seek medical attention for serious burns, burns involving molten material, chemical burns, electrical burns, or burns larger than a minor superficial injury.

### 16.2 Eye Injury

- Stop work immediately.
- Do not rub the eye.
- Use the eyewash station if chemical exposure or particles are suspected and it is appropriate to do so.
- Seek medical attention for solder paste, flux, chemical splash, clipped lead impact, embedded particles, or persistent irritation.
- Report the incident immediately.

### 16.3 Cuts or Punctures

- Stop work.
- Use first aid as appropriate.
- Seek medical attention for deep cuts, punctures from contaminated items, embedded debris, uncontrolled bleeding, or loss of motion or sensation.
- Report the injury immediately.

### 16.4 Fume Exposure or Ventilation Failure

- Stop heating immediately if safe to do so.
- Move away from the fumes.
- Keep the oven closed if safe and useful to contain emissions.
- Turn off the oven if safe to do so.
- Notify the person responsible for the lab or equipment.
- Do not resume until ventilation is restored and the cause is understood.
- Seek medical attention for breathing difficulty, chest tightness, severe irritation, dizziness, headache, nausea, or persistent symptoms.

### 16.5 Fire, Smoke, or Burning Smell

- Stop work immediately.
- Power off or unplug the oven only if safe to do so.
- Alert nearby personnel.
- Keep the oven door closed if opening it would feed a fire and it is safe to leave it closed.
- Use a fire extinguisher only if trained, the fire is small, and there is a clear exit path.
- Evacuate and call emergency services if the fire grows, produces heavy smoke, involves electrical equipment, involves chemicals, or cannot be controlled immediately.
- Do not resume operation until the equipment and work area have been inspected and the cause has been corrected.

### 16.6 Electrical Fault

- Stop work.
- Do not touch exposed wiring, damaged cords, wet equipment, or energized parts.
- Disconnect power only if safe to do so.
- Tag the equipment out of service.
- Notify the person responsible for the lab or equipment.

## 17. Stop-Work Authority

Every employee has authority to stop oven work if they believe conditions are unsafe. Work may resume only when the unsafe condition is corrected and the person handling the oven has confirmed the setup is safe.
